Research Overview (SEB Poster Summary)

This research investigates how temperature shapes the performance, physiology, and population persistence of the harlequin ladybeetle (Harmonia axyridis) by integrating laboratory thermal performance experiments with fine-scale environmental microclimate data.

The aim is to link organismal thermal physiology with real-world environmental conditions to improve predictions of species responses to climate variability and extreme heat events, such as heatwaves.

Key Findings

  • Thermal performance differs substantially across traits (e.g. locomotion vs reproduction).
  • Reproductive traits show a broader thermal tolerance than locomotary or metabolic performance.
  • Exposure to high temperatures reduces reproductive output and physiological performance.
  • Microclimate variation strongly alters predicted realised performance in natural conditions.

Current Research (Details)

My research focuses on how temperature shapes the performance and climate vulnerability of the invasive harlequin ladybeetle (Harmonia axyridis). I combine laboratory experiments with long-term field microclimate data collected around Stellenbosch to understand how insects experience thermal environments in nature.

A key component of this work is comparing thermal performance across multiple traits and integrating laboratory-derived thermal performance curves with fine-scale microclimate data to estimate realised performance under natural conditions.

I also investigate physiological responses to thermal extremes, including damage accumulation, repair processes, and antioxidant protection during simulated heatwaves.

Example thermal performance curve (TPC) from R modelling.

Future Research

I aim to develop integrated thermal ecology models that combine laboratory physiology with fine-scale environmental heterogeneity to improve predictions of species responses to climate change. I also aim to develop models that elucidate the effects of fine-scale microclimate buffering effects on the thermal performance of insects in natural conditions. Finally, I aim to better describe the homeostatic balance between damage and repair mechanisms in insects under extreme temperature events linked to climate change.
