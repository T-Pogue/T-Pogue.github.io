SEB Poster - Research Overview

This research investigates how temperature shapes the performance, physiology, and invasion success of the harlequin ladybeetle (Harmonia axyridis) by integrating laboratory thermal performance experiments with fine-scale environmental microclimate data.

The aim is to link organismal thermal physiology with real-world environmental conditions to improve predictions of species responses to climate variability and extreme heat events.

Key Findings

  • Thermal performance differs substantially across traits (e.g. locomotion vs reproduction).
  • Metabolic rate shows a narrower thermal tolerance than behavioural performance.
  • Exposure to high temperatures reduces reproductive output and physiological performance.
  • Realised performance predictions differ between traits
  • Microclimate variation strongly alters predicted realised performance in natural conditions.

Current Research

My research focuses on how temperature shapes the performance and persistence of the invasive harlequin ladybeetle (Harmonia axyridis). I combine laboratory experiments with long-term field microclimate data collected around Stellenbosch, South Africa, to understand how insects experience thermal environments in nature.

A key component of this work is comparing thermal performance across multiple traits and integrating laboratory-derived thermal performance curves with fine-scale microclimate data to estimate realised performance under heterogenous natural conditions.

I also investigate physiological responses to thermal extremes, including damage accumulation, repair processes, and antioxidant protection during simulated heatwaves.

Future Research

I aim to develop integrated thermal ecology models that combine laboratory physiology with fine-scale environmental heterogeneity to improve predictions of species responses to climate change. I further aim to improve the knowledge of the physiological response of insects to extreme heat events, such as heatwaves, to better predict insect responses to increased climate variability and climate change.
